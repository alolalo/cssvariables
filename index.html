Words of Image: Design tokens as @layers

With the rise of semantical design language in code, we also see parts of graphical application grammar being hauled over to the progamming languages we see today. CSS is one of them that is applying a layering strategy to it’s system design, completely configurable on the user (author) end.

I’m using this metaphorical approach a lot in my system design work (DX) too, so it’s safe to say that layers are here to stay. I like it especially because it explains visually what you see in text, and it’s universal (UX).

Another word that is a layer that can be easily undone is “design tokens”.

Most are just used as variables, but what makes a design token is it’s system-wide application and structuring. The system has a design to it, and so does the naming of it’s parts or it does not corellate in code to begin with. The coded narrative starts with naming things.

This to me is what differs a design language from a programming language; the plane of perception is clearly human, where the plane of a programming language is clearly a computer.

I strictly apply it to creating design tokens for design systems in CSS using the new @layers feature.

So it’s clear to see how the computer loads the design tokens and uses them as a system of design later on. Regardless if it’s inheritable, it makes sure you talk to a developer in their language; design & code.

1. Literal Tokens (@base, -1)

These are the most comon variables you can imagine, but the semantical notation of literal suggests the value is literal rather than the name. Literal tokens therefor have hardcoded values, not relative values like another variable that is stored inside. Literal Tokens are stored in my @layer base.

2. Relative Tokens (@brand, 0)

Relative Tokens do just that; relate to another token for ease of re-usability. These tokens never hold any hard-coded values, and are used as a reference (like a white label). These tokens are stored in my @layer brand.

3. Nested Tokens (@theme, +1)

Nested tokens look a lot alike, and there is a reason for it; they are literal tokens to their equally named relative token. If that’s confusing, imagine a literal token to only serve on specific relative token; the one that is named after itself.

This is the use-case of a nested token; to save extra characters or a namespace needed (just an added _underscore) to show the direct relationship of the token-set, within a set, without losing any relevance to the whole set.

This mostly comes in handy when you need to load them correctly on start, and I got this pattern from Lea Verous article who describes it as “something”. I put them in a seperate @layer theme, which you could put in a higher order than @brand respectively, but they don’t surpass the @base (inheritance, unless none present).

4. Lateral Tokens (@utilities, +2)

Lateral Tokens can have multiple tokens stored inside to make up a selector’s mark-up, like how you can write out HSL-values that have concurrent patterns. These require tokens from multiple layers even, so lateral tokens are specifically used as a utility-layer. This is why I put them in a seperate @layer utility so all the parametrics stay nicely configurable in the head of the design system.

BONUS: JAWS-TOKENS

In my persuit of design language happiness, I came across a pattern that used escaping characters as semantics! The \/\/\/ teeth of JAWS-tokens are what made the name for it obviously.

Why did I create them? Because it worked and then you realize some things...

Why it’s a funny way to make a statement about writing developer-friendly code as mostly a visual spatial learner, since most of that is always considerably... code! Why not graphical code?

Why don’t we make code graphical using only text? Get creative with the copy-writing of things, or do you think CSS actually cares? It just works or not, your browser will tell you.

Because why should you not test your test-colors as if they were brand-colors? Your CSS simply doesn’t care, but it can be used to develop and design in the browser, with the browser.

That to me is actually what a development hand-off should be like in the future; where you layer your system as non-destructively, so they can safely inject and eject your code. And by they I mean everyone in design, development and copywriting.

Everything else is just snotware engineering; trying to untangle the spaghetti an app snorted out. All the design, copy and code is in one pile of dump-code, that doesn’t take your system language into account; it’s spits code, not a design language.

That does not save you any time at all, because what you miss in between is solid documentation and explenation (examples) of how the implementation should work. That’s why you need more management now, because there is no synchronization happening between people yet...

I would rather that you read my own coded narrative, get taken away by a designer who just thinks like Nike and does it, and see how CSS could work as a bridge between design systems. No leadership == leadership.

My deign system methodology, Revolver-DS, was not intended to compete with your DS; it’s intended for prototypical inheritance the right way. And if I ace it, most likely you will just copy what I did. Everything else just doesn’t ship.

That to me is golden because I’m a developer’s best friend, not their replacement. Yet that doesn’t mean I can’t or will not do it if there is simply no design present in their system design.

That is how I intend to use layers because now is the best time to push design into the front-end, and back, without breaking anything. It’s just a text-file, but one that carries all your timely and carefully crafted decisions that should last a lifetime. The source of truth is code, regardless.

And this way I make my design system always naturally retrofit a developer’s DS, from onboarding to offboarding design decissions that need to scale.

They have other intentions for layering their work the way they do. And in hindsight, when you stick to a methodology (Revolver-DS) and a system (CSS), most likely you’re working closest to the “source of truth”; the front-end.
